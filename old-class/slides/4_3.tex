\begin{frame}{Plan}

To describe the notion of a strategy

\begin{frame}{Reading}

Bonanno, section 4.2.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Definition}
\protect\hypertarget{definition}{}
A \textbf{strategy} is a plan for what to do at each information set
where the rules of the game give the player a choice.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Inputs to Strategy}
\protect\hypertarget{inputs-to-strategy}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Strategies are for information sets, not nodes.
\item
  Can't have a plan to do different things at different points in the
  information set.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Comprehensiveness}
\protect\hypertarget{comprehensiveness}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Strategies include plans for what to do at nodes that are ruled out by
  one's earlier choices.
\item
  Reason 1: Fallback.
\item
  Reason 2: Understanding decisions by other players
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategic Form}
\protect\hypertarget{strategic-form}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  We can turn any game, even ones with substantive information sets,
  into a single simultaneous move game.
\item
  Just take the possible moves to be the strategies.
\item
  In any realistic game, there will be a lot of these.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategic Form}
\protect\hypertarget{strategic-form-1}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  But the strategic form can obscure things, as we saw in the case of
  non-credible threats.
\item
  Next time, we'll start looking at how to remove the obscurity.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
